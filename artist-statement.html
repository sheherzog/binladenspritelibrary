Welcome to The Bin Laden Sprite Library! 

For my entire adult life, I have been bothered by the collective memory loss of the Bush Era's impact on our digital present and future. I have marveled at the cyclical head-scratching at how we "got here." Countless articles and documentaries marveling at the ironic to post-ironic detachment of the youth (despite many internet users being well over the age of 20). These critics often classify the Reactionary Internet User as a new species, born fully formed from the ground of 2016, with nothing that came before it.

One of the most frustrating examples of this collective forgetting has been the idolization of 2000s internet culture as somehow both more pure and more cleverly irreverent than that of the Trump Era. In truth, the democratization of the internet very quickly let a "wild west" mentality arise. Hyper-violence in indepedently created games and macro images became the language that both opposed and influenced the approval of the Iraq War. Our depersonalization of SWANA people became incredibly normalized. Simultaneously, our surveillance state began to create the fundamental strategies and tactics we know today are used across the internet (location tracking, astroturfing, wiretapping via the internet and archiving those conversations as "terroristic threats/ideations,"). Originally intended to primarily profile and racially target Muslims and SWANA individuals, this surveillance also led to our own social bigotry and participatory cultures we created with each other to be used against us. This initial expansion of targeted surveillance on the internet by the state led us to where we are today.

When we create these sprites of our biggest monsters and our greatest fears, when we strip them of personhood, post them, and then leave them to rot, do they grow to resent us? Now, as another Forever War rises at the edge of the West's vision, is the time to attach the pieces we left in our wake the first time.

This archive is meant to interoggate the inciting incident of 2000s culture. Here, the 2000s digital cultural response to the September 11th attacks is preserved via flash games, comments, gifs and sprites. This project is an interrogation on "net-stalgia," patriotic violence, islamaphobia and racist dehumanization, as well as the changing tides of a digital culture in its infancy. TW for simulated and real gore, racist language and charicatures, prisoner abuse, and war crimes
